The Semantic Poverty of Open X-Embodiment

Language is the thin leg. A vision-language-action model trains on (vision, language, action) triples. Two of the three are dense: megapixel frames, and continuous control logged at tens of hertz. The third is a single short instruction, held fixed for the whole episode. The largest corpora — Physical Intelligence’s π-series, Google’s RT-2 and Gemini Robotics — are proprietary, so their language cannot be inspected directly; what the papers do report, fixed skill lists and templated commands, points the same way. The one corpus anyone can actually count is the public one.

One sentence, 43% of the data. Open X-Embodiment is the public pool most foundation VLAs draw from. Its largest sub-dataset, kuka, holds 580,392 demonstrations — 42.9% of the release’s 1.35 million — and every one of them carries the same instruction: “pick anything.” It is not a lone case: 40 of the 55 sub-datasets have ten or fewer unique instructions, 18 have exactly one, and those 40 together are 55% of all demonstrations; the median sub-dataset has three. The robot data scales; the language attached to it barely moves.

Too short to reason over. The instructions are tiny as well: a median of 4.4 words per instruction, under six once weighted by how much data each dataset contributes, and two for kuka. At that length there is nothing for the usual generalization tricks to act on — chain-of-thought, step decomposition, and in-context exemplars each need an instruction with some internal structure, and “pick anything” has none. What follows counts that language across all 55 sub-datasets.

1. Counting All 55 Sub-Datasets

How we counted. We pulled the natural-language instruction set of all 55 Open X-Embodiment sub-datasets — including the six that no public mirror carries, taken straight from the original RLDS — deduplicated each one and counted. Trajectories is the number of recorded demonstrations; unique is how many distinct instructions exist in the whole dataset. The pattern is hard to miss: the largest datasets record tens or hundreds of thousands of demonstrations and teach them with a handful of sentences.

Huge data, a handful of sentences. kuka records 580,392 demonstrations and teaches every one of them with the single sentence “pick anything.” Among the large datasets, only Language Table and the newer Bridge export pair instruction variety with their episode count; the rest train hundreds of thousands of demonstrations on a handful of sentences. Counting whole demonstrations — one instruction each — is the honest measure of how many distinct things the robot was ever asked to do.

3. The Prior Finding (Wanna et al., 2026)

Not an artifact of our counting. Wanna et al. measured the same thing on a five-dataset sample and reported it at the frame level: the instruction is stored on every timestep, so RT-1’s 3.7M is its roughly 87,000 episodes times the frames in each. That framing is the training view — a frame is a training sample, so in one epoch the model is shown an instruction 3.7 million times and only 577 of them are different, each sentence repeated on the order of six thousand times. Their headline holds across the robot datasets: fewer than 2% of instructions are unique.
