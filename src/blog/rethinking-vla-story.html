Rethinking the VLA Story

The standard VLA story goes: collect enough robot demonstrations, scale the model, and a generalist household policy falls out — the way it did for language. This series re-examines that story, one assumption at a time. It starts where the story itself starts: the data.

Part 1 — Data

Two questions decide whether "just scale the data" is even on the table: how much data is there, and how diverse is it? Both answers are sobering. The first table puts language and embodied data on a common footing; the second zooms into the one axis the first can only estimate — instruction diversity — using a community audit that actually counted.

"~530 skills" or "~86 tasks" still overstates how varied the language actually is, because those numbers de-duplicate to verbs and skill clusters. The right question is: across the millions of recorded instructions, how many are unique strings? Wanna et al. measured exactly this across widely-used VLA datasets, and the answer is stark — fewer than 2% of instructions are unique; the rest are templated repetitions over a tiny vocabulary.

So the data case for "just scale it" is weak on both axes at once. Volume is four to five orders of magnitude below a single LLM run, and what little there is repeats a few hundred templated commands. The next part turns from data to what people hope will rescue it — a single end-to-end model that learns the shared structure anyway.
